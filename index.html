About Me

Hey — I'm Thomas Zechariah, a Computer Engineering student at Georgia Institute of Technology from Johns Creek, GA. I'm specializing in Computing Hardware & Emerging Architectures (CHEA) and Cybersecurity — two threads that reflect my drive to understand systems from the silicon up.

I came into Georgia Tech after a standout academic run at Northview High School, where I earned perfect 5s across 11 AP exams, was named a National Merit Finalist, and led multiple organizations as president and vice-president. I was also a Dual Enrollment student at Georgia Tech before officially enrolling as a freshman — so I hit the ground running.

I grew up in a multilingual household and I'm fluent in English and Malayalam, with conversational Spanish — a constant reminder that communicating across barriers, whether cultural or technical, is always worth investing in.

Outside of class, I'm on HyTech Racing's Electrical Team, building sensor systems for a formula-style EV from scratch. I also tutor through Knack and have led community organizations for years. Whether it's soldering a transceiver, debugging firmware, or leading a team — I show up fully.

My Path Forward

My long-term goal is to work at the frontier of computing hardware — whether designing secure processor architectures, contributing to post-silicon computing paradigms, or leading security research for critical infrastructure. I want to build the machines that others build on top of.

My background — from scoring 5s across 11 AP exams to interning at a global firmware company to building EV sensor systems — has shown me I perform best when the problem is hard and the stakes are real. To get there:

  • 1Complete CHEA and Cybersecurity threads at Georgia Tech with strong performance in advanced hardware and systems courses.
  • 2Secure at least one hardware-focused internship before graduation — ideally chip design, embedded security, or firmware.
  • 3Continue hands-on work through HyTech Racing, applying classroom knowledge to real engineering problems with real consequences.
  • 4Evaluate MS or PhD programs with strong computer architecture or security labs by junior year.
  • 5Build a professional network through internships, conferences, and hardware-focused organizations.
